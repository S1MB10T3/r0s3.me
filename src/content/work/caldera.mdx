<Intro>
  Caldera is a developer infrastructure company in the web3 space: customers
  launch and operate their own networks on it, the way teams spin up cloud
  environments. I was the sole product designer for eighteen months, across
  four distinct surfaces: Metabridge, the flagship transfer product; the
  embeddable Bridge Widget; Hub Pages, the per-partner landing page product;
  and the Rollup Engine deploy flow and dashboard. Underneath all of it, the
  Katmai design system and a full company rebrand.
</Intro>

<Section title="designsystem">
  ![Katmai design system cover](media/caldera/design-system-cover.webp)

  Katmai is named for a volcanic peninsula in Alaska that contains a literal
  caldera; the vocabulary went all the way down. The system had one job:
  stay unmistakably Caldera while flexing to any customer's brand. Every
  surface, from the widget to hub pages, needed to inherit a partner's
  colors, type, and corner radii without breaking a component.

  <div className="page-media-row">
    ![Core color palette](media/caldera/colors-palette.webp)

    ![Status and semantic colors](media/caldera/colors-statuses.webp)
  </div>

  The color system leaned on semantic tokens instead of raw hex values, so
  swapping in a partner's palette restyled statuses, surfaces, and data viz
  in one move, while Katmai enforced contrast and accessibility rules
  underneath. A partner could not accidentally ship an inaccessible embed.

  To stress-test theming end to end, I built a custom Figma plugin that
  auto-generated the widget across color, radius, shadow, and typography
  variations. Designer-built tooling like this kept the whole team fast:
  once Katmai matured, new product surfaces spun up in days.

  ![Bridge widget theme variations generated by the Figma plugin](media/caldera/widget-variations.webp)
</Section>

<Section title="artdirection">
  ![Caldera rebrand identity spread](media/caldera/rebrand.webp)

  The rebrand had to read as serious infrastructure rather than crypto
  marketing: restrained, systemic, credible to the developer audience
  Caldera was selling to. I led a graphic designer and an external contract
  team, in partnership with internal marketing, to ship the logo, identity,
  marketing site, and social system. The brand also had to coexist with
  every partner's brand inside the widget and hub pages: confident enough to
  hold its own, neutral enough not to compete.
</Section>

<Section title="widget">
  ![Metabridge widget](media/caldera/metabridge.webp)

  Metabridge is a multi-step transfer flow where any error has irreversible
  consequences, serving 30+ networks with true cross-network pairs across
  more than 10 of them. Two audiences had to trust it: end users moving
  value through it, and partner teams embedding it in their own sites.
  Motion was used as a clarity tool, not decoration: consistent flow
  direction and explicit state surfaces showed where the user was, what the
  system was waiting for, and what would happen next. Error states were
  designed for the worst case, so a half-complete transfer read as
  recoverable instead of catastrophic.

  Research fed the roadmap too: structured interviews with active users
  produced a ranked view of network demand that engineering used to
  prioritize integrations.

  ![Bridge builder configuration flow](media/caldera/bridge-builder.webp)

  The bridge builder let partners assemble and theme their own transfer
  widget without a designer in the loop: drop in a logo and a palette,
  preview the result live, and ship an embed that still behaves like every
  other Caldera deployment.
</Section>

<Section title="hub">
  ![Partner hub page template](media/caldera/hub.webp)

  Every partner that launches on Caldera gets a Hub Page: a templated
  landing page with chain status, transfer access, and ecosystem links. The
  design problem is the same family as Shopify storefronts or Notion site
  templates: one product, N customer instances, each needing to feel owned.
  Strong defaults were the most important decision. A partner who configured
  nothing still shipped a credible page on launch day, because the floor is
  where most partners actually live.
</Section>

<Section title="dashboard">
  ![Rollup Engine dashboard](media/caldera/dashboard.webp)

  The Rollup Engine covers two phases of one experience: a deploy flow from
  evaluation through first deployment, and a dashboard for operating what
  you deployed. The hardest problem in the deploy flow was tiering.
  Developers could fully self-serve for low-stakes test environments, while
  production deployments routed to a sales-assisted path designed to feel
  like context-passing rather than a gate: by the time the conversation
  started, the developer's configuration choices were already documented. On
  the self-serve path, a test deployment went from configuration through
  billing to live in about five minutes.

  The dashboard redesign consolidated workflows that previously lived across
  multiple external sites and tools into a single interface. It rolled out
  as the default across every customer account, with positive reception
  from clients.
</Section>

<Postmortem>
  The signal that mattered did not come from usage dashboards. Retail
  transfer traffic in this space is noisy, inflated by airdrop incentives,
  so I weighted the business outcome instead: during my tenure, the
  deployment flow and Metabridge were cited as contributing factors in 3 of
  Caldera's 5 new customer acquisitions. The surfaces I designed became the
  assets BD demoed and closed with, and the dashboard consolidation shipped
  to every account. Choosing which number to trust was itself part of the
  design work.
</Postmortem>
