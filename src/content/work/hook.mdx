<Intro>
  Hook is an options and perps trading platform in the web3 space, and its
  audience was split down the middle:
  experienced derivatives traders who need power and speed, and complete
  newcomers who have never traded an option. Most products pick a side. Hook
  could not. As the sole product designer I owned the full lifecycle: the V1
  interface, the research that followed, the Odyssey product on desktop and
  mobile, the Sayreville Strata design system, and the brand.
</Intro>

<Section title="odyssey">
  ![Odyssey desktop trading interface](media/hook/odyssey-desktop.webp)

  My first months were spent inside V1, the original Options Protocol
  interface. That work mapped where the friction actually lived. The pros did
  not want a simpler product, they wanted a faster one. Newcomers did not
  want walls of upfront education, they wanted the right cue at the moment
  they were about to commit money. And the order ticket was where almost
  everyone got stuck.

  Odyssey grew out of those findings. It was not a facelift of V1: working
  with the engineering team, I built a new product from user interviews and
  journey planning outward. On desktop, the surface stayed dense enough for
  an active trader to scan markets and execute fast, while the order ticket
  carried its own risk readout: breakeven, maximum loss, and implied
  volatility visible before committing, so the nonlinear payoff of an option
  was never a surprise. Position management used progressive
  disclosure: pros expanded to see everything at once, newcomers got the
  same information one click away.

  ![Odyssey mobile PWA flows](media/hook/odyssey-mobile.webp)

  The mobile PWA compressed the desktop flow into a native-feeling app
  without dropping the scaffolding. Where desktop showed information in
  parallel, mobile serialized it, and education surfaced contextually inside
  the flow instead of a separate onboarding. New users learned options while
  they traded, not before.
</Section>

<Section title="designsystem">
  ![Sayreville Strata design system cover](media/hook/design-system-cover.webp)

  Sayreville Strata is the design system underneath all of it, named for a
  Michelle Stuart piece built from layered brick and rag paper. The name set
  the tone: layered, materially specific, tied to a real place. Built on a
  Material UI base and extended into composable primitives, every Hook
  surface, from the order ticket to the marketing site, is a different
  arrangement of the same blocks.

  <div className="page-media-row">
    ![Color palette](media/hook/colors-palette.webp)

    ![Status colors](media/hook/colors-statuses.webp)
  </div>

  Hook's brand color is orange, one of the hardest cases for WCAG contrast
  the moment it touches text on a light background. I tested every approved
  color combination per theme and locked the tokens so each surface passed AA
  without losing the brand. The guidelines documented the reasoning behind
  each decision, not just the values, so the system stayed coherent as new
  contributors joined.
</Section>

<Section title="branding">
  ![Perps launch teaser](media/hook/perps-teaser.webp)

  Options products default to the terminal-dark aesthetic: dense monospace on
  black, serious and for insiders only. Hook needed to read as a rigorous
  financial product while staying open to first-timers, so the brand went
  the other way: orange, editorial type, and newspaper texture, a
  print-inspired identity that felt approachable and exacting at once.

  Brand strategy ran alongside product design, not before or after it.
  Stakeholder sessions surfaced what the team wanted Hook to be, research
  showed what it needed to say, and the design system enforced the result
  across product and marketing.

  ![Print-inspired brand applications](media/hook/branding.webp)
</Section>

<Postmortem>
  Hook was end-to-end ownership from one seat: research, product, system,
  and brand. The Odyssey launch doubled daily active users, lifted retention
  47 percent, and set the company's single-day volume record at three times
  the previous peak. The frame I would revisit is the pro-versus-newcomer
  binary itself: real users moved between those modes as they learned, and
  the next iteration should adapt to observed behavior instead of asking
  people to pick a lane.
</Postmortem>
